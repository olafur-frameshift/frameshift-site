The claim that prenatal acetaminophen use causes autism has become one of the most politically amplified questions in pharmacoepidemiology, despite an evidence base that has only gotten thinner under scrutiny.

A new research letter in JAMA Pediatrics by Prahm et al. followed 1.5 million Danish children through nationwide registers. Adjusted hazard ratio for autism after prenatal exposure: 1.03 (95% CI: 0.95 to 1.12). The sibling-matched analysis, among the strongest confounding controls available outside a randomised trial, returned 1.09 (0.91 to 1.27). No dose-response. No trimester effect.

This is not a new pattern. A 2024 Swedish cohort of 2.5 million children found sibling HRs of 0.98. A 2025 BMJ umbrella review of 40 studies concluded that associations consistently disappeared in sibling-controlled designs. A signal that does not survive familial confounding is the fingerprint of a confounded association, not a causal one.

Decisions about medication in pregnancy should follow evidence, not warnings issued ahead of it.
